# How to review the HubLab prototype

Thanks for taking a look. This is an early, clickable prototype of the HubLab site — a
single home for our science & engineering engagement content, with public pages for
families (FunLab), young people (FutureLab) and adults (LifeLab), plus the behind-the-scenes
admin workflow.

**A few things to know before you click around:**

- **It's a prototype.** All the content is made up to show the shape of things — names,
  events, ratings and view counts are placeholder. Design polish, real copy and real data
  come later.
- **Nothing you do is permanent or shared.** The prototype runs entirely in your own
  browser, so you can click anything — submit a test item, approve it, delete things — without
  affecting anyone else. If it gets messy, open **Admin → Reset demo data** to start fresh.
- **Buttons that "leave" the site** (Book, Watch, Download, paper links) are placeholders and
  won't go anywhere yet — that's expected.

**What's worth looking at:**

1. **Home** — the overall feel, the three audience routes, and how events/featured items surface.
2. **Find stuff** — filtering by who it's for, age, subject and type, and the search.
3. **What's on** — the events calendar.
4. **Open any item** — each content type (event, video, activity, research explainer, …) has
   its own page layout.
5. **FunLab / FutureLab / LifeLab** — each audience's own themed page. Notice items can appear
   in more than one when they suit more than one audience.
6. **Admin** — the internal side: the submission form, the moderation queue (approve / send
   back / reject), the expiry reminders, and the history log on each item.

**What we'd love your steer on:**

- Does the plain-language, audience-first tone feel right for each group?
- Is anything hard to find, or named confusingly?
- For the team who'll run it: does the admin workflow (submit → review → publish → expire)
  match how you'd actually want to work?

You don't need to install anything — just open the link. Jot notes as you go; rough reactions
are as useful as detailed ones.
